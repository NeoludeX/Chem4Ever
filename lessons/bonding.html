Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

Bonds form because atoms reach a lower energy state together than apart. The type of bond and the shape of the molecule determine nearly every physical and chemical property of a substance.

🔗 Types of Chemical Bonds

Bond TypeElectronegativity DifferenceWhat Happens
Nonpolar Covalent0 – 0.4Electrons shared equally
Polar Covalent0.4 – 1.7Electrons shared unequally; partial charges form
Ionic> 1.7Electron transferred; full charges form

✏️ Lewis Structures

Lewis structures show how valence electrons are distributed. Follow these steps every time:

  1. Count total valence electrons (add 1 per negative charge; subtract 1 per positive charge).
  2. Place the least electronegative atom in the center (never H).
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds (2e⁻ each).
  4. Fill octets on outer atoms with lone pairs first.
  5. Place leftover electrons on the central atom.
  6. If the central atom still lacks an octet, convert lone pairs into double or triple bonds.
Formal Charge = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻) Best structure = minimizes formal charges; negative FC on more electronegative atom
Example: Draw the Lewis structure of SO₃ Total valence e⁻: S(6) + 3×O(6) = 24 e⁻
S is central. Three S=O double bonds uses 24 e⁻ with 2 lone pairs on each O. S has 0 formal charge, each O has 0 formal charge. ✓
Expanded octets are allowed for Period 3+ elements (like S, P, Cl) because they have available d orbitals. Examples: PCl₅ (10e⁻ on P), SF₆ (12e⁻ on S).

🔄 Resonance

When two or more valid Lewis structures differ only in where electrons (not atoms) are placed, those are resonance structures. The actual molecule is a resonance hybrid — an average of all structures. Delocalized electrons lower the molecule's energy and make it more stable than any single structure suggests.

Classic examples: O₃, NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, SO₃, benzene (C₆H₆)

AP Tip: In resonance structures, bond lengths are intermediate between single and double bonds. The O–O bond length in O₃ is between a single (148 pm) and double (121 pm) bond.

📐 VSEPR Theory & Molecular Geometry

🔀 Hybridization

Hybridization explains how atomic orbitals mix to form new equivalent bonding orbitals. Count the electron groups on the central atom to determine hybridization.

Electron GroupsHybridizationExample
2spBeCl₂, CO₂, C₂H₂
3sp²BF₃, SO₃, C₂H₄
4sp³CH₄, NH₃, H₂O
5sp³dPCl₅
6sp³d²SF₆
Sigma (σ) vs Pi (π) bonds: Every single bond = 1 σ bond. A double bond = 1 σ + 1 π. A triple bond = 1 σ + 2 π. Rotation around a bond is only free in σ bonds — π bonds lock the molecule in place (this is why cis/trans isomers exist in alkenes).

🧲 Molecular Polarity

A molecule is polar only if it has both polar bonds AND an asymmetric shape so the bond dipoles don't cancel.

  • Polar: H₂O (bent), NH₃ (trigonal pyramidal), HCl (linear, one end is H)
  • Nonpolar: CO₂ (linear, dipoles cancel), CCl₄ (tetrahedral, dipoles cancel), BF₃ (trigonal planar, dipoles cancel)
