Topic 1

Atomic Structure & Properties

The foundation of all chemistry. Understand how atoms are built, how electrons are arranged, and what that arrangement means for every chemical property.

The Subatomic Particles

Every atom consists of three fundamental particles. The nucleus sits at the center and contains protons and neutrons. Electrons orbit in a cloud around the nucleus.

ParticleChargeMass (amu)Location
Proton+11.0073Nucleus
Neutron01.0087Nucleus
Electron−10.000549Electron cloud

The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons — this defines what element an atom is. The mass number (A) equals protons + neutrons.

Key Relationships Atomic number (Z) = number of protons Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons Neutrons = A − Z Neutral atom: electrons = protons

Isotopes and Average Atomic Mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons. For example, Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons and Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons — but both have 6 protons. The average atomic mass on the periodic table is a weighted average based on natural abundance.

Average Atomic Mass Formula Average mass = Σ (fractional abundance × isotope mass) Example: Cl-35 (75.77%) and Cl-37 (24.23%) = (0.7577 × 34.969) + (0.2423 × 36.966) = 35.45 amu

Mass spectrometry is the experimental technique used to determine isotope masses and abundances. On the AP exam, you may be given a mass spectrum and asked to calculate average atomic mass from the peaks.

Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals arranged in shells (energy levels) and subshells. The four subshell types are s, p, d, and f, each with a specific shape and maximum electron capacity.

SubshellOrbitalsMax Electrons
s12
p36
d510
f714

Three rules govern how electrons fill orbitals:

  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first (1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d...)
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. Each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule: Within a subshell, electrons occupy separate orbitals singly before pairing up (maximizes spin).
Worked Example: Write the electron configuration of Iron (Fe, Z=26)

Fill orbitals in order: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶

Condensed (noble gas) notation: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶

Note: Fe has 6 electrons in the 3d subshell — two orbitals are paired, three are singly occupied (Hund's rule).

Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

PES measures the energy required to remove electrons from different subshells. The resulting spectrum shows peaks corresponding to each occupied subshell. Higher binding energy = closer to nucleus = harder to remove.

📌 AP Exam Tip: PES is frequently tested. Peak height ∝ number of electrons in that subshell. The highest binding energy peak corresponds to the innermost electrons (1s). Be able to identify the element from a PES spectrum and connect it to electron configuration.

Moles and Molar Mass

The mole is chemistry's counting unit. One mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). Molar mass (g/mol) numerically equals atomic mass (amu) but at the macroscopic scale.

Mole Conversions moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol) particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³ moles = particles ÷ 6.022 × 10²³
Worked Example

How many atoms are in 10.0 g of calcium (Ca, molar mass = 40.08 g/mol)?

Moles of Ca = 10.0 ÷ 40.08 = 0.2495 mol

Atoms = 0.2495 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 1.50 × 10²³ atoms

Topic 2

Periodic Trends

The periodic table is organized so that trends in atomic properties are predictable. Understanding why these trends exist is just as important as knowing the patterns themselves.

Effective Nuclear Charge (Z_eff)

Electrons in inner shells shield outer electrons from the full pull of the nucleus. The effective nuclear charge felt by a valence electron is approximately:

Effective Nuclear Charge Z_eff ≈ Z − S Z = atomic number, S = shielding constant (≈ number of inner-shell electrons)

As you move across a period (left to right), Z increases but electrons are added to the same shell (poor shielding), so Z_eff increases. This is the root cause of most periodic trends.

Atomic Radius

Atomic radius measures the size of an atom. Two opposing forces control it: Z_eff pulling electrons inward vs. the number of electron shells pushing outward.

  • Across a period (→): Radius decreases. Z_eff increases, pulling electrons closer to the nucleus.
  • Down a group (↓): Radius increases. New electron shells are added, increasing distance from nucleus.
📌 Largest atom: Cesium (Cs) — bottom-left of the table. Smallest atom: Helium (He) — top-right (noble gases excluded from comparisons, use Fluorine for smallest reactive atom).

Ionization Energy (IE)

Ionization energy is the energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom. Higher Z_eff and smaller atomic radius both increase IE.

  • Across a period (→): IE generally increases (higher Z_eff, electrons harder to remove)
  • Down a group (↓): IE decreases (outer electrons are farther from nucleus)

Important exceptions: IE dips from Be→B (removing from 2p is easier than 2s) and from N→O (O has a paired 2p electron that experiences extra repulsion, making it easier to remove).

Successive Ionization Energies

Each successive electron removed requires more energy (fewer electrons, same nuclear charge = stronger pull). A dramatic jump in successive IEs signals you've reached a core electron shell — this reveals the element's group number.

Example: An element with successive IEs of 738, 1450, 7733, 10,540... The big jump after the 2nd IE means the element has 2 valence electrons → Group 2 (Magnesium).

Electron Affinity (EA)

Electron affinity is the energy change when a neutral gaseous atom gains an electron. More negative EA = greater tendency to gain electrons. Generally increases (becomes more negative) across a period and up a group. Halogens have the highest electron affinities.

Electronegativity

Electronegativity measures an atom's ability to attract bonding electrons toward itself. Uses the Pauling scale (F = 4.0, highest).

  • Increases across a period (→) and up a group (↑)
  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element (3.98)
  • Cesium/Francium are the least electronegative

Ionic Radii

When atoms form ions, their radius changes significantly:

  • Cations (lose electrons) are smaller than the parent atom — fewer electrons, same nuclear charge = stronger pull per electron
  • Anions (gain electrons) are larger than the parent atom — more electrons, same nuclear charge = weaker pull per electron
⚠️ Common AP Trap: Na⁺ and F⁻ are isoelectronic (both have 10 electrons), but Na⁺ (Z=11) is smaller than F⁻ (Z=9) because higher Z means stronger pull on the same 10 electrons.

Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

Bonds form because atoms achieve lower potential energy together than apart. The type and geometry of bonds determine virtually every physical and chemical property of a substance.

Types of Chemical Bonds

Bond TypeElectronegativity DifferenceDescription
Nonpolar Covalent0 – 0.4Equal sharing of electrons
Polar Covalent0.4 – 1.7Unequal sharing; partial charges (δ+/δ−)
Ionic> 1.7Electron transfer; full charges

Lewis Structures

Lewis structures show how valence electrons are distributed in a molecule. Follow these steps:

  1. Count total valence electrons (add 1 per negative charge; subtract 1 per positive charge)
  2. Connect atoms with single bonds (2e⁻ each)
  3. Complete octets on terminal atoms first (except H, which needs only 2)
  4. Place remaining electrons on central atom
  5. If central atom lacks octet, convert lone pairs to multiple bonds
📌 Formal Charge: FC = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻). The best Lewis structure minimizes formal charges (ideally all zero). If charges unavoidable, negative FC should be on the more electronegative atom.

Resonance

When a molecule can be drawn with multiple valid Lewis structures differing only in the placement of electrons (not atoms), those structures are resonance structures. The actual molecule is a resonance hybrid — an average of all structures. This delocalization of electrons lowers energy and makes the molecule more stable.

Classic examples: O₃ (ozone), NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, benzene (C₆H₆)

VSEPR Theory and Molecular Geometry

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts shape: electron groups (bonds + lone pairs) arrange themselves to maximize distance from each other.

Unit 1

Atomic Structure & Properties

Everything in chemistry begins with atoms. This unit covers what atoms are made of, how electrons are arranged, and how the periodic table organizes elements by their properties. You'll also master the mole — chemistry's fundamental counting unit.

🔬 Atomic Structure

Atoms consist of three subatomic particles: protons (positive charge, in nucleus), neutrons (no charge, in nucleus), and electrons (negative charge, orbiting nucleus).

  • Atomic number (Z): number of protons — defines the element
  • Mass number (A): protons + neutrons
  • Isotopes: same element, different number of neutrons (e.g., C-12 and C-14)
  • Ions: atoms that have gained or lost electrons (anion = gained e⁻, cation = lost e⁻)
Atomic mass (amu) = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)
Example: Chlorine is 75.77% Cl-35 (34.969 amu) and 24.23% Cl-37 (36.966 amu).
Avg mass = (0.7577 × 34.969) + (0.2423 × 36.966) = 35.45 amu

⚛️ Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals arranged in energy levels (shells). The order of filling follows the Aufbau principle: fill lowest energy orbitals first.

Filling order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p...

Two additional rules govern electron arrangement:

  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in the same orbital can have the same spin — each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule: When filling orbitals of equal energy (like three 2p orbitals), put one electron in each before doubling up.
Example: Iron (Fe, Z=26)
Full: 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶4s²3d⁶
Abbreviated: [Ar] 4s²3d⁶
⚠️ Watch out: Cr and Cu are exceptions — they steal an electron from 4s to get a half-filled or fully-filled 3d subshell, which is more stable. Cr = [Ar]4s¹3d⁵, Cu = [Ar]4s¹3d¹⁰

📡 Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

PES measures the energy required to remove electrons from an atom. Each peak in a PES spectrum corresponds to a subshell. Higher binding energy = closer to nucleus = harder to remove. The relative height of each peak reflects the number of electrons in that subshell.

AP Tip: You'll be asked to match a PES spectrum to an element. Count the peaks (= number of subshells) and relative heights (= number of electrons per subshell).

📊 Periodic Trends

TrendAcross Period (→)Down Group (↓)
Atomic RadiusDecreasesIncreases
Ionization EnergyIncreasesDecreases
ElectronegativityIncreasesDecreases
Electron AffinityGenerally increasesGenerally decreases

Why? Going across a period, nuclear charge increases while shielding stays roughly the same, pulling electrons closer (smaller radius, higher IE). Going down a group, new electron shells are added, increasing distance from nucleus and shielding.

⚖️ The Mole & Molar Mass

The mole is the chemist's counting unit. One mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). Molar mass (g/mol) = the mass of one mole of a substance, numerically equal to its atomic/molecular mass in amu.

moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol) particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³
Example: How many moles are in 54 g of water (H₂O)?
Molar mass of H₂O = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 g/mol
moles = 54 ÷ 18.016 = 3.00 mol

Molecular & Ionic Compound Structure

This unit is about how atoms bond together and what shape the resulting molecules take. Shape matters — it determines polarity, reactivity, and physical properties. Master Lewis structures and VSEPR theory here.

🔗 Types of Chemical Bonds

Bond TypeBetweenHow It Works
IonicMetal + NonmetalElectron transfer; forms lattice structure
CovalentNonmetal + NonmetalElectron sharing
MetallicMetal + Metal"Sea" of delocalized electrons

Electronegativity difference determines bond character: <0.4 = nonpolar covalent; 0.4–1.7 = polar covalent; >1.7 = ionic.

✏️ Lewis Structures

Lewis structures show how valence electrons are arranged in a molecule. Follow these steps:

  1. Count total valence electrons from all atoms.
  2. Place the least electronegative atom in the center.
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds (use 2 electrons each).
  4. Fill octets on outer atoms first with lone pairs.
  5. Give remaining electrons to the central atom.
  6. If the central atom lacks an octet, form double or triple bonds.
Example: CO₂
C has 4 valence e⁻, each O has 6 → total = 16 e⁻
Structure: O=C=O (two double bonds, no lone pairs on C, 2 lone pairs on each O)
Formal Charge: = Valence e⁻ − Lone pair e⁻ − ½(Bonding e⁻)
The best Lewis structure minimizes formal charges. A negative formal charge should be on the more electronegative atom.

📐 VSEPR Theory & Molecular Geometry

Electron groups (bonds + lone pairs) repel each other and arrange to be as far apart as possible. This determines molecular geometry.

Electron GroupsLone PairsShapeBond Angle

31Bent~117°

41Trigonal Pyramidal~107°

Key insight: Lone pairs repel MORE than bonding pairs. This is why water (2 lone pairs) has a bond angle of ~104.5° — less than the tetrahedral 109.5°.

🧲 Molecular Polarity

A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds AND an asymmetric geometry. Even if bonds are polar, a symmetric molecule (like CO₂ or CCl₄) is nonpolar because the bond dipoles cancel.

  • Polar: H₂O, NH₃, HCl, SO₂
  • Nonpolar: CO₂, CH₄, BF₃, N₂

🔀 Hybridization

Hybridization explains how atomic orbitals mix to form new equivalent orbitals for bonding.

Electron GroupsHybridizationExample
2spBeCl₂, CO₂
3sp²BF₃, C₂H₄ (each C)
4sp³CH₄, NH₃, H₂O
5sp³dPCl₅
6sp³d²SF₆

Sigma (σ) bonds are formed by head-on overlap (all single bonds are σ). Pi (π) bonds are formed by side-by-side overlap (the 2nd bond in a double bond and 2nd+3rd in a triple bond are π).

Unit 3

Intermolecular Forces & States of Matter

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are the attractions between molecules — weaker than bonds but responsible for physical properties like boiling point, melting point, viscosity, and solubility. This unit also covers gases, solutions, and phase diagrams.

🤝 Types of Intermolecular Forces (weakest → strongest)

IMFBetweenStrength
London Dispersion (LDF)All moleculesWeakest (increases with molar mass)
Dipole-DipolePolar moleculesModerate
Hydrogen BondingN–H, O–H, or F–HStrong (special dipole-dipole)
Ion-DipoleIon + polar moleculeVery strong
AP Rule: Higher IMF strength → higher boiling point, higher melting point, lower vapor pressure. When comparing molecules, identify the strongest IMF each has, then compare those.
Example: Why does water boil at 100°C but H₂S at −60°C despite H₂S being heavier?
Water forms hydrogen bonds (strong); H₂S only has dipole-dipole forces. H-bonds dominate, giving water a much higher boiling point.

💨 The Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT P = pressure (atm), V = volume (L), n = moles, R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K, T = temperature (K)

An ideal gas has no intermolecular forces and negligible particle volume. Real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature.

  • Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T, n)
  • Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P, n)
  • Avogadro's Law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (constant P, T)
  • Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...
Example: A 2.0 L container holds 0.50 mol of gas at 300 K. What is the pressure?
P = nRT/V = (0.50)(0.08206)(300)/2.0 = 6.15 atm

🌡️ Phase Diagrams

A phase diagram shows which state (solid, liquid, gas) a substance exists in at given temperature and pressure.

  • Triple point: all three phases coexist in equilibrium
  • Critical point: above this temperature and pressure, there is no distinct liquid/gas phase — the substance becomes a supercritical fluid
  • Normal boiling point: where the liquid-gas line crosses 1 atm
Water is unusual: Its solid-liquid line has a negative slope — meaning increasing pressure at constant temperature can melt ice. This is because ice is less dense than liquid water.

🧪 Solutions & Concentration

Molarity (M) = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

"Like dissolves like": polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes.

Colligative properties depend on the number of dissolved particles, not their identity:

  • Boiling point elevation: ΔTb = Kb × m × i
  • Freezing point depression: ΔTf = Kf × m × i

where m = molality (mol solute/kg solvent) and i = van't Hoff factor (number of particles per formula unit).
