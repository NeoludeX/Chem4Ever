Thermodynamics I — Energy & Enthalpy

Thermodynamics is the study of energy and its transformations. This lesson covers heat, enthalpy, calorimetry, and Hess's Law — the tools used to measure and calculate energy changes in chemical reactions.

🔥 Heat, Temperature & Energy

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles. Heat (q) is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures. Heat flows from hot → cold.

  • Exothermic reaction: releases heat to surroundings; q < 0; ΔH < 0 (e.g., combustion)
  • Endothermic reaction: absorbs heat from surroundings; q > 0; ΔH > 0 (e.g., melting ice)
q = mcΔT q = heat (J) m = mass (g) c = specific heat capacity (J/g·°C) ΔT = T_final − T_initial Specific heat of water = 4.184 J/g·°C
Example: How much heat is needed to raise 200 g of water from 25°C to 75°C?
q = 200 × 4.184 × (75 − 25) = 200 × 4.184 × 50 = 41,840 J = 41.84 kJ

🧪 Calorimetry

A calorimeter measures heat flow during a reaction. In a coffee-cup calorimeter (constant pressure), heat lost by reaction = heat gained by solution.

q_reaction = −q_solution = −mcΔT ΔH = q_reaction ÷ moles of limiting reagent
Example: 50.0 mL of 1.00 M HCl is mixed with 50.0 mL of 1.00 M NaOH. Temperature rises from 22.0°C to 28.9°C. Find ΔH per mole of water formed. (Assume density = 1.00 g/mL, c = 4.184 J/g·°C)
mass = 100.0 g; ΔT = 6.9°C
q_solution = 100.0 × 4.184 × 6.9 = 2887 J
q_reaction = −2887 J; moles H₂O = 0.0500 mol
ΔH = −2887 ÷ 0.0500 = −57,740 J/mol = −57.7 kJ/mol

📏 Enthalpy & Standard Enthalpies

Enthalpy (H) is the heat content of a system at constant pressure. We measure ΔH — the change in enthalpy. Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔH°f) is the enthalpy change when 1 mole of a compound forms from its elements in standard states.

ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants) Note: ΔH°f of any pure element in its standard state = 0
Example: Calculate ΔH°rxn for: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)
ΔH°f values: CH₄ = −74.8, O₂ = 0, CO₂ = −393.5, H₂O(l) = −285.8 kJ/mol
ΔH°rxn = [−393.5 + 2(−285.8)] − [−74.8 + 0]
= [−965.1] − [−74.8] = −890.3 kJ/mol

🔗 Hess's Law

Enthalpy is a state function — it doesn't matter what path you take, only where you start and end. Hess's Law lets you add reactions algebraically to find ΔH for a target reaction.

Rules when manipulating reactions:

  • If you reverse a reaction, flip the sign of ΔH
  • If you multiply a reaction by a factor, multiply ΔH by the same factor
Example: Find ΔH for C(s) + ½O₂(g) → CO(g) given:
(1) C + O₂ → CO₂ ΔH = −393.5 kJ
(2) CO + ½O₂ → CO₂ ΔH = −283.0 kJ
Reverse reaction (2): CO₂ → CO + ½O₂ ΔH = +283.0 kJ
Add to (1): C + O₂ + CO₂ → CO₂ + CO + ½O₂
Cancel CO₂: C + ½O₂ → CO ΔH = −393.5 + 283.0 = −110.5 kJ

🔩 Bond Enthalpies

You can estimate ΔH by counting bonds broken (endothermic) and bonds formed (exothermic).

ΔH ≈ Σ(bond energies broken) − Σ(bond energies formed) Breaking bonds: + (endothermic) Forming bonds: − (exothermic)
AP Tip: Bond enthalpy calculations give estimates — actual ΔH values use standard enthalpies of formation and are more accurate. The AP exam may ask you to use either method.
