Topic 12

Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry connects chemical reactions to electrical energy. Galvanic cells generate electricity from spontaneous redox reactions; electrolytic cells use electricity to drive nonspontaneous ones. This unit ties together redox, thermodynamics, and equilibrium.

🔄 Oxidation & Reduction (Redox)

Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons. Remember: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

  • Oxidation: loses electrons → oxidation number increases
  • Reduction: gains electrons → oxidation number decreases
  • Oxidizing agent: causes oxidation; gets reduced itself
  • Reducing agent: causes reduction; gets oxidized itself
Assigning Oxidation Numbers: Pure elements = 0; monatomic ions = their charge; O is usually −2; H is usually +1; sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = the ion's charge.

⚖️ Balancing Redox Equations (Half-Reaction Method)

  1. Split into oxidation and reduction half-reactions
  2. Balance all atoms except H and O
  3. Balance O by adding H₂O
  4. Balance H by adding H⁺ (acidic) or OH⁻ (basic)
  5. Balance charge by adding electrons (e⁻)
  6. Multiply half-reactions so electrons cancel
  7. Add half-reactions together and simplify
Example (acidic solution): Balance MnO₄⁻ + Fe²⁺ → Mn²⁺ + Fe³⁺
Reduction: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O
Oxidation: Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻ (×5)
Overall: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5Fe²⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O + 5Fe³⁺

🔋 Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

A galvanic cell converts chemical energy from a spontaneous redox reaction into electrical energy. It has two half-cells connected by a wire and a salt bridge.

  • Anode: oxidation occurs; negative electrode in galvanic cell
  • Cathode: reduction occurs; positive electrode in galvanic cell
  • Salt bridge: maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ion flow
  • Electrons flow from anode → cathode through the external wire
E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (using standard reduction potentials from the table) Spontaneous cell: E°cell > 0
Example: Zn/Zn²⁺ || Cu²⁺/Cu cell.
E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V (cathode — higher reduction potential)
E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V (anode)
E°cell = 0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V ✓ (positive = spontaneous)

📉 The Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation calculates cell potential under non-standard conditions (concentrations ≠ 1 M).

E = E° − (RT/nF) ln Q At 25°C simplified: E = E° − (0.0592/n) log Q n = moles of electrons transferred Q = reaction quotient
Example: For the Zn/Cu cell, if [Zn²⁺] = 2.00 M and [Cu²⁺] = 0.100 M, find E.
Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu; n = 2; E° = 1.10 V
Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] = 2.00/0.100 = 20.0
E = 1.10 − (0.0592/2) log(20.0) = 1.10 − 0.0388 = 1.061 V

⚡ Electrolytic Cells

Electrolytic cells use external electrical energy to drive nonspontaneous redox reactions (E°cell < 0). Used for electroplating and industrial processes like making aluminum.

  • Anode is connected to the positive terminal of the power source
  • Cathode is connected to the negative terminal
  • Oxidation still at anode; reduction still at cathode
Faraday's Law: moles of e⁻ = (current × time) / F = (I × t) / 96,485 I = current in amperes (A) t = time in seconds
Example: How many grams of Cu deposit when 2.00 A flows for 30.0 min through CuSO₄ solution?
moles e⁻ = (2.00 × 1800) / 96,485 = 0.03731 mol
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu, so moles Cu = 0.03731/2 = 0.01866 mol
mass = 0.01866 × 63.55 = 1.19 g Cu
