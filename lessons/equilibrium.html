Topic 9

Equilibrium

Most reactions don't go to completion — they reach a state of equilibrium where forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate. Mastering equilibrium is essential for acids/bases, solubility, and thermodynamics.

⚖️ The Equilibrium Constant (Keq)

At equilibrium, the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations is constant — the equilibrium constant K.

For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]^c [D]^d / [A]^a [B]^b Rules: • Pure solids and pure liquids are OMITTED from K expressions • K > 1: products favored at equilibrium • K < 1: reactants favored at equilibrium • K = 1: roughly equal amounts of both
Kp vs Kc: Kp uses partial pressures (atm); Kc uses molar concentrations (M). They relate by: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn, where Δn = moles gas products − moles gas reactants.

🧮 ICE Tables

ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) tables organize equilibrium calculations. This is the most important skill in this unit.

Example: H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), Kc = 49.0 at 450°C.
Initial: [H₂] = 1.00 M, [I₂] = 1.00 M, [HI] = 0

         H₂    +    I₂    ⇌    2HI
I:     1.00       1.00        0
C:    −x        −x        +2x
E:  1.00−x    1.00−x     2x

Kc = (2x)² / (1.00−x)² = 49.0
Take square root: 2x/(1.00−x) = 7.00
2x = 7.00 − 7x → 9x = 7.00 → x = 0.778
[HI] = 2(0.778) = 1.556 M; [H₂] = [I₂] = 0.222 M

📊 Reaction Quotient (Q)

Q has the same form as K but uses current (non-equilibrium) concentrations. Comparing Q to K tells you which direction the reaction will proceed.

If...Then...
Q < KReaction shifts RIGHT (toward products) to reach equilibrium
Q = KSystem is already at equilibrium
Q > KReaction shifts LEFT (toward reactants) to reach equilibrium

↔️ Le Châtelier's Principle

When a system at equilibrium is stressed, it shifts to partially counteract that stress.

Stress AppliedSystem Response
Add reactantShifts right (toward products)
Remove reactantShifts left (toward reactants)
Add productShifts left
Increase pressure (decrease volume)Shifts toward fewer moles of gas
Increase temperature (exothermic rxn)Shifts left; K decreases
Increase temperature (endothermic rxn)Shifts right; K increases
Add catalystNo shift; reaches equilibrium faster
⚠️ A catalyst does NOT change K or the equilibrium position — it only speeds up how quickly equilibrium is reached.

🧂 Solubility Equilibria (Ksp)

The solubility product constant (Ksp) describes the equilibrium between an ionic solid and its dissolved ions.

For PbCl₂(s) ⇌ Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq): Ksp = [Pb²⁺][Cl⁻]² (Pure solid PbCl₂ is omitted)
Example: Ksp of AgCl = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰. Find molar solubility.
AgCl ⇌ Ag⁺ + Cl⁻
Let solubility = s: Ksp = s × s = s² = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰
s = √(1.8×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.34×10⁻⁵ M
Common Ion Effect: Adding an ion that's already in the equilibrium shifts it left, decreasing solubility. Example: AgCl is less soluble in NaCl solution than in pure water because Cl⁻ is already present.
