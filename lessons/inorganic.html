Topic 13

Inorganic Chemistry

Inorganic chemistry covers the properties and reactions of non-carbon-based compounds — metals, ionic compounds, coordination complexes, and the chemical behavior of elements across the periodic table.

🏷️ Naming Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds form between metals (cations) and nonmetals (anions). Naming follows specific rules:

  • Name the cation first, then the anion
  • For metals with multiple oxidation states, use Roman numerals: Fe²⁺ = iron(II), Fe³⁺ = iron(III)
  • Monatomic anions end in -ide: Cl⁻ = chloride, O²⁻ = oxide
  • Polyatomic oxyanions: memorize NO₃⁻ (nitrate), SO₄²⁻ (sulfate), PO₄³⁻ (phosphate), CO₃²⁻ (carbonate), OH⁻ (hydroxide), NH₄⁺ (ammonium)
Examples:
FeCl₃ → iron(III) chloride
Cu₂SO₄ → copper(I) sulfate
Ca(NO₃)₂ → calcium nitrate

🔵 Transition Metals & Coordination Compounds

Transition metals form coordination compounds by accepting electron pairs from ligands (Lewis bases). The metal acts as a Lewis acid.

  • Coordination number: number of ligands bonded to the metal
  • Common ligands: H₂O, NH₃, Cl⁻, CN⁻, OH⁻
  • Chelating ligands (polydentate) bind through multiple atoms — EDTA is a classic example
Naming coordination compounds: 1. Name ligands alphabetically (with prefixes di-, tri-, etc.) 2. Name the metal with its oxidation state in Roman numerals 3. If the complex is an anion, add -ate suffix to the metal name Example: [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ = tetraamminecopper(II) ion
Color in transition metal complexes: Transition metals absorb certain wavelengths of visible light. The color you see is the complementary color to the one absorbed. This is why Cu²⁺ solutions are blue and Cr³⁺ solutions are green.

📋 Solubility Rules

Knowing which ionic compounds dissolve in water is essential for predicting precipitation reactions and writing net ionic equations.

Generally SolubleExceptions (Insoluble)
All nitrates (NO₃⁻)None
All Group 1 and NH₄⁺ saltsNone
All chlorides (Cl⁻)AgCl, PbCl₂, Hg₂Cl₂
All sulfates (SO₄²⁻)BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄
—Most carbonates, phosphates, hydroxides, sulfides are insoluble (except Group 1 and NH₄⁺)
Net Ionic Equation Example:

Full ionic: Pb²⁺ + 2NO₃⁻ + 2K⁺ + 2I⁻ → PbI₂(s) + 2K⁺ + 2NO₃⁻

Net ionic: Pb²⁺(aq) + 2I⁻(aq) → PbI₂(s)
