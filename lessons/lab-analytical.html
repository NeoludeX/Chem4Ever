Lab & Analytical Chemistry

Lab skills and analytical techniques are tested directly on the AP exam — both in free-response questions and through data interpretation. This lesson covers the techniques, measurements, error analysis, and spectroscopy you need to know.

📏 Significant Figures & Measurement

Every measurement has uncertainty. Significant figures communicate the precision of a measurement.

  • All nonzero digits are significant
  • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant (e.g., 1.008 has 4 sig figs)
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant (0.0045 has 2 sig figs)
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal ARE significant (2.500 has 4 sig figs)
Multiplication/Division: answer has same sig figs as least precise measurement Addition/Subtraction: answer has same decimal places as least precise measurement
AP Tip: Always report your final answer with the correct number of significant figures. Losing points for sig figs on free-response is very common and very avoidable.

⚠️ Errors in Experiments

Understanding error types is regularly tested in AP free-response questions.

Error TypeDefinitionEffect
Random ErrorUnpredictable variation in measurementsAffects precision; scatter around true value
Systematic ErrorConsistent bias in one directionAffects accuracy; all values shifted same way
  • Accuracy: how close a measurement is to the true value
  • Precision: how reproducible measurements are (consistency)
  • You can be precise without being accurate (systematic error), or accurate on average without being precise (random error)
Percent Error = |experimental − theoretical| / theoretical × 100%

🔬 Titration

Titration is a technique where a solution of known concentration (the titrant) is added to a solution of unknown concentration until the reaction is complete (the equivalence point). An indicator changes color at the endpoint.

At equivalence point: moles of acid = moles of base (for 1:1 reactions) M_acid × V_acid = M_base × V_base
Example: 25.00 mL of HCl is titrated with 0.150 M NaOH. The equivalence point is reached after adding 32.47 mL of NaOH. Find the molarity of HCl.
moles NaOH = 0.150 × 0.03247 = 0.004871 mol
moles HCl = 0.004871 mol (1:1 ratio)
M_HCl = 0.004871 ÷ 0.02500 = 0.195 M

Titration curves show pH vs volume of titrant added. Key features to know:

  • Strong acid + strong base: equivalence point at pH = 7
  • Weak acid + strong base: equivalence point at pH > 7 (conjugate base is basic)
  • Weak base + strong acid: equivalence point at pH < 7
  • The half-equivalence point of a weak acid titration: pH = pKa

🌈 Spectroscopy & Beer's Law

Spectrophotometry measures how much light a solution absorbs. The more concentrated the solution, the more light it absorbs. This allows you to determine unknown concentrations.

Beer-Lambert Law: A = εlc A = absorbance (no units) ε = molar absorptivity (L/mol·cm) l = path length (cm) c = concentration (mol/L) Absorbance is also related to transmittance: A = −log(T) = −log(I/I₀)
Example: A solution has an absorbance of 0.630 at 520 nm. Using a calibration curve, this corresponds to a concentration of 0.0315 M. Beer's Law is most accurate when A is between 0.1 and 1.0.
Calibration Curve: Plot absorbance (y-axis) vs known concentration (x-axis) for several standard solutions. Draw a best-fit line. Then use the line equation to find unknown concentrations from measured absorbance values. This is standard AP free-response territory.

🧫 Chromatography

Chromatography separates mixtures based on how strongly components interact with a stationary phase vs a mobile phase.

  • Paper/TLC chromatography: compounds travel different distances based on polarity. More polar compounds travel less far on a nonpolar stationary phase.
  • Rf value: Rf = distance traveled by compound ÷ distance traveled by solvent. Rf is characteristic for each compound under the same conditions.
  • Gas chromatography (GC): separates volatile compounds by boiling point and affinity for stationary phase. Each peak in a chromatogram = one component.
Rf = distance traveled by spot / distance traveled by solvent front Range: 0 to 1 (no units)

📊 Graphical Analysis & Data Interpretation

The AP exam frequently gives you data sets and asks you to analyze them. Key skills:

  • Identify the slope and intercept of a best-fit line and connect them to physical quantities
  • Recognize linear relationships (e.g., ln[A] vs t for first-order kinetics, 1/[A] vs t for second-order)
  • Calculate rate of change from a graph (slope = Δy/Δx)
  • Identify outliers and explain sources of error
  • Distinguish between correlation and causation
AP Free-Response Tip: When asked to "justify" or "explain" an experimental result, always connect your answer to a specific chemical principle — don't just describe what happened, explain why it happened at the molecular level.
