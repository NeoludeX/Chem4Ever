Topic 5

Solutions & Intermolecular Forces

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures where one substance dissolves into another. Whether something dissolves, and how much, depends on intermolecular forces. This lesson covers IMFs, solubility, concentration, and colligative properties.

🤝 Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

IMFs are attractions between molecules (not within them). Stronger IMFs = higher boiling point, higher melting point, lower vapor pressure.

IMFPresent InRelative Strength
London Dispersion (LDF)All moleculesWeakest; increases with molar mass & surface area
Dipole-DipolePolar moleculesModerate
Hydrogen BondingMolecules with N–H, O–H, or F–H bondsStrong (special dipole-dipole)
Ion-DipoleIon + polar solventVery strong; drives ionic dissolving
Why does water boil at 100°C but H₂S at −60°C?
H₂S is heavier, but water has O–H bonds → hydrogen bonding. H₂S only has dipole-dipole forces. Water's H-bonds are far stronger, requiring much more energy to break → much higher boiling point.

💧 Solubility: "Like Dissolves Like"

Polar solvents (like water) dissolve polar and ionic solutes. Nonpolar solvents (like hexane) dissolve nonpolar solutes. The driving force is forming favorable IMFs between solvent and solute.

  • NaCl dissolves in water: ion-dipole forces between Na⁺/Cl⁻ and H₂O
  • Oil does not dissolve in water: oil is nonpolar; no favorable IMFs with water
  • Iodine (I₂) dissolves in hexane: both nonpolar; LDF interactions

📊 Concentration Units

Molarity (M) = mol solute ÷ L solution ← most common on AP Molality (m) = mol solute ÷ kg solvent ← used for colligative properties Mole fraction (χ) = mol A ÷ total mol solution
Molarity vs Molality: Molarity changes with temperature (volume changes); molality does not (mass is constant). Always use molality for boiling point elevation and freezing point depression.

🌡️ Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend on the number of dissolved particles, not their identity. Adding a solute always raises the boiling point and lowers the freezing point.

Boiling Point Elevation: ΔTb = Kb × m × i Freezing Point Depression: ΔTf = Kf × m × i m = molality (mol solute / kg solvent) i = van't Hoff factor (particles per formula unit) Kb(water) = 0.512 °C/m Kf(water) = 1.86 °C/m

The van't Hoff factor (i): nonelectrolytes (sugar, glucose) → i = 1. Electrolytes dissociate: NaCl → i = 2, CaCl₂ → i = 3.

Example: What is the freezing point of a solution made from 90.0 g of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆, MM = 180 g/mol) in 500 g of water?
m = (90.0/180) ÷ 0.500 = 1.00 mol/kg; i = 1 (nonelectrolyte)
ΔTf = 1.86 × 1.00 × 1 = 1.86°C
Freezing point = 0 − 1.86 = −1.86°C
