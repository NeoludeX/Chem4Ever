Topic 1

Atomic Structure & Properties

Everything in chemistry begins with atoms. This lesson covers what atoms are made of, how electrons are arranged, how to read photoelectron spectra, and how to use the mole — chemistry's fundamental counting unit.

⚛️ Subatomic Particles

Every atom has a dense nucleus containing protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral), surrounded by a cloud of electrons (negative). The number of protons defines the element.

🔬 Isotopes & Average Atomic Mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons (same Z, different A). The average atomic mass on the periodic table is a weighted average based on natural abundance.

Average atomic mass = Σ (fractional abundance × isotope mass)
Example: Chlorine Cl-35 is 75.77% abundant (34.969 amu); Cl-37 is 24.23% abundant (36.966 amu).
Avg mass = (0.7577 × 34.969) + (0.2423 × 36.966) = 35.45 amu
AP Tip: Mass spectrometry produces a spectrum with peaks at each isotope mass. Peak height = relative abundance. You may be asked to calculate average atomic mass directly from a mass spectrum.

🌀 Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals in shells and subshells. Three rules govern how they fill:

  • Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest-energy orbitals first.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: Each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule: Fill each orbital in a subshell singly before pairing (like seats on a bus).
Filling order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d...

📡 Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

PES fires high-energy photons at an atom and measures how much energy is needed to eject electrons from each subshell. The result is a spectrum of peaks.

  • Higher binding energy = electrons closer to nucleus (core electrons)
  • Lower binding energy = valence electrons, easier to remove
  • Peak height is proportional to the number of electrons in that subshell
AP Tip: Given a PES spectrum, count the peaks to find the number of occupied subshells. Use peak heights to determine how many electrons are in each. Then match to an electron configuration to identify the element. This is tested almost every year.

⚖️ The Mole & Molar Mass

The mole is the chemist's counting unit. One mole contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). Molar mass (g/mol) numerically equals the atomic/molecular mass in amu.

moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol) particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³ mass = moles × molar mass
Example 1: How many moles are in 90 g of water (H₂O)?
Molar mass = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 g/mol
moles = 90 ÷ 18.016 = 5.00 mol
Example 2: How many molecules are in 5.00 mol of H₂O?
molecules = 5.00 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 3.01 × 10²⁴ molecules
