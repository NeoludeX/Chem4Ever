Topic 2

Periodic Trends

The periodic table isn't just an organizational chart — it's a map of atomic behavior. Every trend has a root cause. Understand why trends exist and you'll never need to memorize them.

🎯 Effective Nuclear Charge (Z_eff)

Electrons in inner shells shield outer electrons from the full pull of the nucleus. The net pull a valence electron actually feels is the effective nuclear charge.

Z_eff ≈ Z − S Z = atomic number (total protons) S = shielding constant ≈ number of core (inner shell) electrons

Going left to right across a period, Z increases by 1 each step but electrons are added to the same shell (poor shielding of each other), so Z_eff increases. This one fact explains most periodic trends.

Example: Na (Z=11) has Z_eff ≈ 11 − 10 = 1. Cl (Z=17) has Z_eff ≈ 17 − 10 = 7. Chlorine's valence electrons are pulled much more strongly toward the nucleus.

📏 Atomic Radius

Atomic radius is the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron. Two factors compete: nuclear pull drawing electrons inward, and additional electron shells pushing outward.

  • Across a period (→): Decreases. Z_eff increases, pulling electrons closer. Same number of shells.
  • Down a group (↓): Increases. New electron shells added, increasing average distance from nucleus despite higher Z.
Largest atom: Cesium (Cs) — bottom-left. Smallest non-noble atom: Fluorine (F) — top-right.

⚡ Ionization Energy (IE)

The first ionization energy is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from a neutral gaseous atom. Higher Z_eff and smaller radius both mean electrons are harder to remove.

  • Across a period (→): Generally increases. Z_eff increases, electrons are held tighter.
  • Down a group (↓): Decreases. Outer electrons are farther from nucleus and more shielded.

Important exceptions across Period 2:

  • IE drops from Be → B: Boron removes a 2p electron (higher energy, easier) vs. beryllium's 2s.
  • IE drops from N → O: Oxygen has a paired 2p electron; electron-electron repulsion in the pair makes it easier to remove one.
Successive Ionization Energies (Key AP Skill):
Each successive IE is higher (fewer electrons, same nuclear charge = stronger pull per electron).
A dramatic jump in successive IEs signals you've crossed into a core shell — this reveals the group number.

Example: IEs of 496, 4,562, 6,912 kJ/mol → giant jump after 1st IE → 1 valence electron → Group 1 (sodium)

🧲 Electronegativity

Electronegativity measures an atom's ability to attract shared (bonding) electrons toward itself. Uses the Pauling scale (F = 4.0).

  • Increases across a period (→) — higher Z_eff pulls bonding electrons closer
  • Decreases down a group (↓) — valence electrons farther from nucleus
  • Most electronegative: F (3.98) → O (3.44) → N/Cl (~3.0)
  • Least electronegative: Cs / Fr (~0.7)
AP Connection: The electronegativity difference between two bonded atoms determines bond type: <0.4 = nonpolar covalent, 0.4–1.7 = polar covalent, >1.7 = ionic.
